Stages of Stellar Evolution

The stellar journey is a continuous battle between gravity pulling inward and nuclear fusion pushing outward.

Stage 1

Stellar Nebula

The nurseries of stars. Colossal clouds of cold hydrogen gas and cosmic dust drift in space. Triggered by a shockwave or gravitational disturbance, regions of the cloud begin to collapse under their own gravity.

Stage 2

Protostar

As the collapsing pocket of gas grows denser, its core heats up rapidly. Friction and gravitational compression raise temperatures to millions of degrees, glowing dimly as it prepares to ignite fusion.

Stage 3

Main Sequence

The core hits 15 million Kelvin, igniting hydrogen fusion. The star achieves hydrostatic equilibrium, where outward radiation pressure perfectly balances inward gravity. This is the longest and most stable phase.

Stage 4

Red Giant Expansion

When hydrogen runs dry, the core contracts and heats up, while the outer layers expand dramatically and cool, turning red. Low-mass stars become Red Giants; high-mass stars swell into massive Red Supergiants.

Stage 5

Supernova / Nebula Death

Low-mass stars gently shed their outer layers, creating gorgeous Planetary Nebulae. Massive stars go out in a violent, universe-shaking Supernova explosion, synthesizing heavy elements and blasting them into space.

Stage 6

The Cosmic Remnant

What remains at the center. Medium stars leave behind dense, glowing White Dwarfs. High-mass stars collapse into ultra-dense Neutron Stars or, if massive enough, infinite gravitational points: Black Holes.
